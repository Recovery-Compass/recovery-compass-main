## Legal Problem: Billing for Own Fee Motion

### Question of Law
Should a client pay for attorney's work to collect fees from the client?

### Industry Standard: NO

**Reasoning:**
1. **Self-Serving:** Attorney is working for own benefit, not client's
2. **Conflict of Interest:** Fee motion is adverse to client's interests
3. **Unreasonable:** Client shouldn't pay for attorney's fee collection efforts
4. **Against Public Policy:** Creates perverse incentive (more work = more fees to collect)

### Case Law Support
While no California case directly addresses this, analogous principles:

**Rule 1.5 (Reasonable Fees):**
> Fee must be reasonable considering "the time and labor required" for **client's benefit**

**Keech Declaration purpose:**
> To support attorney's fee collection, not provide legal service to client

## Strategic Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: Post-Termination Work Unreasonable
> Kirk billed $630 for drafting his own fee motion on November 6, 2025—**eight days after Nuha terminated his representation**. This work provided no benefit to Nuha; it was self-serving fee collection effort. The fee request should be reduced by $630 at minimum.

### Argument 2: Borson Inapplicable
> *Borson* allows attorney to seek fees from **opposing party** when client terminates without cause. Here, Nuha terminated for cause:
> - Kirk ignored October 26 email (Rule 1.4 violation)
> - Kirk filed unauthorized motions October 27
> - Kirk's communication failures justified termination
> 
> When termination is for cause, *Borson* relief is inappropriate.

### Argument 3: Fees Were Unreasonable Throughout
> Even before post-termination work, Kirk's fees were unreasonable due to:
> - Block billing (15+ entries)
> - Vague descriptions (10+ entries)
> - Excessive time (35-52% above standards)
> - Communication failures (Rule 1.4 violations)
> 
> The fee request should be reduced to justified amount of $6,000-8,000, representing work actually performed for Nuha's benefit.
